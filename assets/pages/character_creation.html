Step 2: Determine Character Motivation And Burdens

Burdens represent the debts a Player Character owes. Defined simply, they are among the main reasons a Player Character is a Shadowrunner in the first place. These debts may include money owed, binding contracts, responsibilities, addictions or similar that the character holds in high regard. A character's actions can often be guided by his Burdens and can have very tangible effects in their development.

Over the course of a campaign, unresolved burdens can put pressure on characters. Additional burdens may be given out by the Gamemaster, awarding them goods and services that would normally be out of reach for them. This provides players with a choice; do they resolve their burdens as quickly as possible, do they maintain their current burdens, or do they take on even more in the hopes that the risks will pay off in the future? Additionally, Gamemasters can hand out burdens as consequences for bad choices, or just plain bad luck; maybe a Player Character gets blackmailed by a rival runner for a job gone bad, or they suffer an addition due to prolonged substance abuse.

Starting Burdens

Each Player Character starts with at least one Burden. Each player should work together with their Gamemaster in incorporating these Burdens into the character's backstory and the setting itself. Burdens be as elaborate part or the character's main Motivations - such as a detailed relocation of how the character was an involuntary test subject who escaped a secret corporate laboratory and is actively hunted by said corporation - or quite vague - such as simply having kids - as long as these Burdens can be used to add personal stakes to a Player Characters life in the shadows.

Each Burden consists of two parts:

  • A title: This does not have any direct rules effects, but is intended to offer an explanation that allows the player to work the Obligation into their character's story.
  • A size This is the Burden's size, and determines the mechanical effects of a Burden. The size of each players starting Burdens is based on the starting number if players, as determined in the table below:

Rules for Creating Custom Archetypes

When creating an archetype, each character gains ten archetype skills. These skills are associated with your characters general career, and taking ranks in these are generally cheaper than taking ranks in non-archetype skills.

When creating a custom Archetype for your character, choose 6 general skills that define the general archetype of the character. Then, choose 4 more skills, and you...

  • ...must have at least 1 social or combat skill,
  • ...must have at least 1 knowledge skill,
  • ...can’t have more than 3 combat skills (Hacking, Spellcasting, Summoning and Resonance all count as a combat skill),
  • ...can’t have more than 3 social skills,
  • ...can’t have more than 4 knowledge skills,
  • ...can’t have more than 2 additional general skills

These skills broadly represent a specialization of your character within its base archetype.

Magical skills have some further restrictions: When taking a magical skill as an archetype skill, you must pay 15 Karma for each skill, and you cannot take Resonance as a magical archetype skill if you have taken any other magical archetype skill. Additionally, when gaining any Magical skill except Resonance, your character gains the Awakened trait, or the Emergent trait if Resonance was taken as an archetype skill.

Step 6: Determine Derived Attributes

Once you've spent your character's starting Karma, the next step is to determine your character's derived attributes:

Wound Threshold: Your character's wound threshold represents how much physical damage they are able to withstand before becoming incapacitated. Your character's starting wound threshold is determined by adding their metatypes base wound threshold to their Brawn rating. After you determine this initial value, further increases of your Brawn do not increase your wound threshold.

Strain Threshold: Your character's strain threshold how much mental stress your character can withstand before becoming stunned, dazed or otherwise incapacitated. Your character's strain threshold is determined by adding their metatypes base strain threshold to their Willpower rating. After you determine this initial value, further increases of your Willpower do not increase your strain threshold.

Soak: Your character's soak value determines how much damage they can shrug off every time they're hit. When your character suffers damage - usually by getting hit by an attack - you subtract your character's soak value from the damage inflicted, before applying the remaining damage against your wound or strain threshold.

Your character's base soak value is equal to their Brawn rating. After you determine this initial value, further increases to your character's Brawn rating do increase their soak value. Further increases in your character's soak limit are often applied by gear, most commonly by bodytech and armor, and talents. Usually, multiple sources of armor are not cumulative, unless specifically specified.

Defense: Your character's defense represents their extra levels in protection that makes them harder to hit in combat. Defense is divided into melee defense and ranged defense. Your character's starting defense is 0, and typically raised through equipment and talents.

Each point of defense adds j to all attacks coming towards your character; melee defense thereby protects your character against swords and punches, while ranged defense protects your character from gunfire, thrown knifes and most ranged spells. Unless otherwise specified, defense from different sources do not stack - for example, a Barrier spell granting your character one point of defense does not add to the point of defense already gained through a worn Armor Jacket - but effects that explicitly add to your defenses - such as the Defensive and Deflective item qualities - do stack with your inherit defense. Additionally, the maximum amount of defense a character can have is 4.

System Strain: Your character's system strain represents how much damage your persona - your digital manifestation in the Matrix - can receive before shattering. A shattered persona renders you offline and unable to interact with the Matrix in any way, until your persona is repaired. Usually, your system strain threshold is 8, and can be increases with enhancements to your commlink, cyberdeck or RCC.

However, if your character is Emergent - for example by having Resonance as an archetype skill at character creation - you do not have a system strain threshold; instead, your strain threshold becomes your system strain threshold.

Firewall: Firewall determines how well your persona is protected from hacking and tempering. Your character's firewall behaves as equal part Soak and Defense against hacking attempts against you. Firewall is most commonly gaines through gear and talents, and usually starts at 0.

Essence: Essence is the colloquial term for a measurement of the physical and spiritual integrity of a living being. Sometimes referred to as the soul, the ka or the chi of a being, the holisticity of one's essence determines their humanity - the ability to relate and empathize others and oneself - as well as the connection to the mana surrounding them. Heavy surgical work, such as implanted bodytech, substance abuse, life-changing traumatic experiences and attacks by essence-stealing critters such as vampires can cause irreversible Essence loss, and thereby disrupt a beings connection to Magic, and others.

By default, every living being has an Essence loss threshold of 7; if the character's essence loss reaches this threshold, the character dies. Essence loss has two consequences: For one, since the character has literally lost part of their humanity, every non-hostile social skill check they make adds j for every two points of Essence loss a character suffers from. Additionally, every point of Essence loss upgrades the difficulty of any Magical skill check a character makes, and if the Essence loss is ever higher than a magically active character's Willpower characteristic, they burn out, losing access to any and all magical skills and powers, forever.

Essence loss is usually irreversible, with the only known exception being an unfathomably expensive and decades-long recovery therapy, which requires the removal of all cybernetic enhancements in a persons' body. If a character decides to remove a piece of bodytech, or if a point of essence loss is caused by something other than bodytech, a character does not heal Essence loss, but they gain an Essence hole, which can be filled by cybernetic augmentations before suffering any further Essence loss.

Step 7: Choose Gear

At this point, the players should have taken care of most of the mechanical details of their characters. Now, it is time to determine the gear your character uses in order to use their skills and abilities in their shady day-to-day dealings. For this, each character starts with a budget of ¥500, as well as a set of basic everyday clothing, a standard commlink, and some gear that allows then to interact with the Matrix wirelessly; remember that this budget can be increased by taking an additional Burden. Before their characters begin the game, players may spend this money to purchase items as they please. However, they may not purchase Restricted (R) items unless specifically given approval from the Gamemaster.

Characters keep any unspent money when they start the game. In addition, once a player has finished spending their starting cash, they roll 1d100; this counts as the character's "pocket money", and cannot be spent during character creation.
